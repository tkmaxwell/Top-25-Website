Love Where You're At

Allen Stone: Love Where You're At

Allen Stone: Radius

Allen Stone has always been a purest and appreciative of music in its most organic form, music that makes you groove. Soul music has always hit all those marks: "Soul music is more of a feeling than it is a sound. Soul exists in a tone and an authenticity...I wanted to get into soul music because of the way it felt, which was very authentic." His obsession with and comparison too soul's greatest, that include Stevie Wonder, Al Green, and Prince, have made him a sweater bearing hippie who appreciates retro and is hesitant to dive in to where music is today.

He even fights that notion in "Fake Future" by encouraging us to "chuck our laptops" and ask: "What good is my microphone if I don't really sing?/ What good is my music if it ain't really me?" However, the part that makes Radius great is that Stone never clings too tightly to the past and wonderfully toes the line of paying homage in a fresh and new way.

"The radius is the distance between the center point of a circle to its exterior," Stone explains. "This record really is a description, a definition, a correlation - whatever you want to call it - between the center of me and my being, to my exterior." This line perfectly encapsulates the album because for any of us there is a wide range of emotions circling around our inner and outer selves and this is no different for Stone.

The aforementioned "Fake Future" finds Stone worried about technology. "Circle" speaks of being trapped in hopelessness and fear. "Where You're At" asks us all to appreciate the good things that we have. All of these topics sit atop a historical lesson on soul music with the added touch of that fancy technology that Stone treats with trepidation. Vocally, Stone is as crisp as ever as his voice soars per usual, carrying over that charisma that was gained as a kid spending his days leading the choir for his father's church. When asked about what his goal was for the record Stone stated: "When I set out to make this record I really wanted to find my own individual spectrum of music. I think I've found it. It's this weird, white funk meets Northwest soul." Take one listen and that odd sentence will make perfectly good sense

The Ghosts of Beverly Drive

Death Cab For Cutie: The Ghosts of Beverly Drive

Death Cab For Cutie: Kintsugi

The Japanese practice of Kintsugi is a ceramic process in which fragmented pieces are melded together with liquefied or dusted metals like gold and silver. The end product obviously depends on the original state of the fragmented pieces, but ultimately the fractured pieces make for a more beautiful, structurally sound whole. Kintsugi the album is Ben Gibbard and Death Cab For Cutie's attempt at finding their end piece of art.

17 years into their career there has always been an overarching theme of growing up in Death Cab's unique brand of indie. Over the years it has evolved from the overinflated moments of young age into the nuanced moments of adulthood. No matter what stage in life there will always be a sense of trying to find yourself and establish meaningful connections. Couple those themes with the departure of founding member Chris Walla and, for Gibbard personally, the very public divorce with Zooey Deschanel and you certainly have your fragmented pieces. And to keep the metaphor going the music is the liquefied metal needed to create the new piece of art! My goodness I get it now!

There is a certain voyeuristic uneasiness, very similar to Bjork's Vulnicura, when you hear lines like "Darling, though you may pretend that you are selfless/You break with but the slightest bend and it leaves you lost and helpless" or "You'll never have to hear the word 'no'/If you keep all your friends on the payroll." The beauty of music is that you can choose to interpret it your own way. "Little Wanderer" finds the protagonist hoping to be a beacon to a globe-trotting lover. Is that about Zooey? Probably, but it doesn't have to be. The songs also stand perfectly as their own narrative and offer more than any Hollywood love story can offer. Ultimately, Kintsugi is an album about drifting apart and a band trying to stay together. It is the many moments of survival that we come across over and over again as we get older. The next step is to take the pieces and make something beautiful out of it.

Clearest Blue

CHVRCHES: Every Open Eye

CHVRCHES: Every Open Eye

When a band launches from obscurity and releases a fully-formed and dynamic debut album there is always a cautious hesitancy about what they may do next. In 2013, CHVRCHES jumped out of Glasgow and straight on top of the 80's synth-pop wave. The Bones of What You Believe was confident, piercing, and furious. The trio relentlessly toured the album over the better of two years and in that time their crowds and stages grew bigger and they grew more confident as they found their legs as live performers.

Artists often take different routes to avoid the dreaded "sophomore slump" to various results. Some can be too ambitious, not ambitious enough, or experiment with a new sound trying to capture the magic the launched them into the spotlight. CHVRCHES decided that they wanted to take everything they learned on the road and stay exactly as they were. The result is Every Open Eye and it takes every sincere, big, and melodic hallmark of Bones and injects a new confidence and efficiency.

CHVRCHES have crafted an album for the live audience. They have figured out what works and took it to the max. The hooks are big and clean. The songs never rush, but perfectly approach their climax. Let's take a look at album highlight "Clearest Blue." It has no standard pop structure. You won't find a chorus, but what you will find is an ultra-confident Lauren Mayberry whose vocals soar with a fragile tension as the lush arrangement dances around her with an ever increasing build. And then the coveted drop hits you, not in a cheesy EDM manner, but in a surging, monumental, keyboard riff that channels Depeche Mode. These moments are all over the album. From the waste-no-time opener "Never Ending Circles" or the glittery sparkle of "Leave a Trace" to the way in which Mayberry stretches out the word gold in "Make Them Gold."

Speaking of Mayberry, there is a complete confidence in her vocals and delivery. Early on in CHVRCHES' run Mayberry struggled to find her way on stage and the trials that come from being a popular female in the music industry. Over the last two years she has not only gained her chops on stage, but has relentlessly come after misogynistic online trolls and audience hecklers. Not letting anyone get away with has gone on too long in the business. To see this confidence come full form in this album is what makes it so good. You keep doing you CHVRCHES and we will continue to ride along.

Ship to Wreck

Florence and the Machine: How Big, How Blue, How Beautiful

Florence and the Machine: How Big, How Blue, How Beautiful

Florence Welch is an absolute force. Both in her voice, ambition, and performances on stage. It seemed like the only person capable of stopping Florence and her Machine was Florence herself. And that is exactly what happened at this year's Coachella when she jumped off the stage and broke her foot. As painful and frustrating as I am sure that was for Welch, especially considering she was just starting to promote the album, it forced her to relax for just a little bit of time. In the weeks after the injury she resorted to a stool for performances and the songs were re-arranged to follow suit.

All of this is to say that I believe the injury helped us prepare ourselves for Welch's best and most complete album. There are the triumphant and dominant moments that the Machine has been known for, but there is a new eloquence and fragility. On previous albums it seems like there was grandeur for the sake of grandeur and none of that is present here. A lot of that is due to producer Markus Dravs (Arcade Fire, Bjork, Coldplay). He wanted to strip these songs down as much as possible and pick his spots of impact. You see this play out in the immediacy of the acoustic drums and backbeat of "Ship to Wreck" or the electronic atmospherics that begin "What Kind of Man" before it launches into a chunky guitar riff and arena sized drum kick.

The appearance of more mellow tracks helps with the fact that this is a relationship record for Welch and very introspective. "Various Storms and Saints" and "Long and Lost" are low key while the former veils commentary on her relationship through biblical reference as she is apt to do while the latter is completely straight forward. Even the title track is mellow before the career defining moment when the Goldfrapp led 36 piece string and brass section takes it to new heights.

I had the pleasure of seeing Florence both during the broken foot and after it was healed. The first performance gave a new and different life to her music that shows itself on this album. I am not sure if it is in her plans, seeing as performance two she was a maniac and all over the place, but if she ever did decide to do an acoustic tour it would be a joy to hear these songs played in that format.

Change is Everything

Son Lux: Change is Everything

Son Lux: Bones

Ryan Lott, the mastermind behind all things Son Lux, keeps himself a busy man. The innovative, classically trained, beat-making, composer has spent his time creating imaginative and complex music across all mediums. He has scored for TV, film, dance, and collaborated with the likes of Beyonce producer Boots, Sufjan Stevens, and Richard Reed Parry of Arcade Fire. All that to say that the man has a lot of ideas in his head and he uses Son Lux to help get those ideas out.

A solo endeavor in the past, Lott has hired his live companions guitarist Rafiq Bhatia and drummer Ian Chang for Bones. This was a fantastic move on Lott's part because having seen Son Lux three times I can say Chang and Bhatia are some of the most unique musicians out there today. The newly formed trio has allowed Son Lux to push boundaries even further than before.

At the onset, Son Lux music is in no way easy listening. A mind like Lotts simply doesn't construct traditional song frameworks. Bones dives deep into dark waters. The grab bag of genre and influence gets mixed around until it becomes controlled chaos. It is often times something you can't wrap your head around, but will certainly get stuck in there anyways. The music toes the line between electronic jazz pop and classical arrangements. The constant struggle between the two adds an additional character to the music that isnâ€™t seen in other types of music. Trust me when I say the hooks are all over the place, it just takes some time to find them.

Having musicians in the studio this time around has created a sense that they are adapting in real time to the artificial instruments and choppy samples that Lott throws out at them. It's as if Lott is saying "Alright here comes this shift from minimalism to bombastic maximialism, let's see what they do with this." "This Time" is Latin-meets-indsutrial in which Chang bounces syncopated drums off of the machine like backbeat. All to be capped off by a nine-person choir because why not? "Undone" finds Rafiq using slick, soulful, jazz guitar runs.

Lott's vocals are often times sparse for good reason. He takes the vocals-as-instrument approach that aims to slot in with the music instead of being laid on top of it. Lyrically he speaks introspectively about life and personal longing. Lead single "Change is Everything" is an awesome play on words discussing the tiny moments in life that become major turning points. Speaking of that song, I want to close with demanding you look at the linked video for the song. It is stop motion from drawings created with pins and strings. It is unbelievable and I don't understand how people have the time and patience to pull it off. Enjoy.

When We Were Young

Adele: When We Were Young

Adele: 25

Adele is officially queen of the world. I talked to some people and confirmed this. Seeing as this album is a super-mega-ginormous critical success there isn't much value added in discussing the details of the songs and music because everyone has already heard it many times over. As a result I will use this space to do some free form discussion on craziness of this album and its impact on the music industry.

As of this writing Adele's first week numbers have just come out and they are officially listed at a whopping 3.38 million copies! This blows away the previous mark set by N*SYNC's 2.42 million in sales of No Strings Attached. To see these numbers in 2015 is just something truly remarkable. In a time where singles and curated playlists dominate the music scene, not to mention the availability of streaming and single song downloads, how was Adele able to pull this off?

To me there are many reasons for this. The first is that the album is really good and Adele has a once in a lifetime voice. The second and probably biggest factor is that Adele is a cross-generational, cross-cultural, cross-genre etc. etc. talent. Her music is cool enough for the millennials and hipsters and safe enough for the soccer moms to pick up at Target and spin on the way to AYSO. Why do you think the latest Now compilation shows up on the billboard top whatever? It is because there is still a ton of people (likely older) who don't have time for Spotify or iTunes and come across their music during their weekly trip to WalMart. If you couple that with the millennials who can't find the album on Spotify or Apple Music and have now bought their second album in five years (Taylor Swift being the first) you have 3.38 million copies! It is crazy how much dominance this album has had to all forms of music listeners in such a small amount of time.

To briefly touch on the subject of her keeping it off streaming services it was much more of a business decision than anything. Despite what some people have written, I don't think it marks any significant shift in people buying albums or the demise of streaming. These cases are few and far in between. The fact that One Direction, Bieber, and Adele crushed sales for two weeks doesn't indicate a seismic shift. It is just statistics people. Anomalies happen. Adele will eventually put the albums on streaming after sales die down and if anything this is the tactic that might gain traction. If you want to hear the music when it comes out you will have to pay a premium or else you wait until later. It is no different than seeing a movie in theaters vs. waiting for it to drop onto Netflix. For 99% of artists streaming is another one of many options not previously available to make money. I completely agree they don't make enough from these services (that should change with time) and album sales are down, but there is money to be made. In 2015 you can make it without a label, make money off numerous physical media, make money on social media, make money off streaming etc. etc.

If I can add anything to the music itself it would be to take a look at the co-writers on the album. It is always fun with big albums like this to discover who helped write the music and listen to it through a new lens. "Send My Love (To Your New Lover)" is co-written by mega-producer Max Martin. "River Lea" features Danger Mouse. Personal favorite "When we were Young" is with Tobias Jesso Jr. who you will hear from shortly. Throw open the Wikipedia and there is all types of cool stuff you can discover about the writing process. While you do that Adele will continue to reign supreme on this land of ours.

Father's Day

Butch Walker: Father's Day

Butch Walker: Afraid of Ghosts

Over the years there are 5 songwriters that I have become absolute favorites of mine. My for-no-reason-at-all criteria include:

1) They go by their own name

2) If not exclusively by their name they must have had multiple bands

3) Are a part of my era of music listening

2015 has been a banner year for all those songwriters. Andrew McMahon released a 10 year version of Everything in Transit and re-issued a deluxe version of his In the Wilderness debut while Ryan Adams released a behemoth Live at Carnegie Hall Album and a let's-not-get-into-it cover album of Taylor Swift. Neither one of them released new material so you won't find it on this list but the other two will be found later on this list (how about that for drama?). To round out the top 5 is the rock and roller himself Butch mother effing Walker.

If you aren't familiar with Butch Walker then a) you have a lot of work to do and b) it is important to know that he is a musical shape shifter. He started his career fronting the hair metal and power-pop bands SouthGang and Marvelous 3 before moving onto a prolific solo career producing top-40 hits and releasing albums on his own. His solo albums have ranged from country and alternative to glam rock and 80's hair metal ballads. His producer credits include songs for Katy Perry, Weezer, Saosin, Gavin DeGraw, Pink, Taylor Swift, Fall Out Boy and on and on and on. Butch works on whatever interests him and is damn good at it.

At the crux of his ever-changing sound is that Walker is a superb songwriter and Afraid of Ghosts finds Walker at his most vulnerable, subdued, and reflective. Produced by whom else but Ryan Adams the album is largely based on the death of his father and his reflection of life as a result. Ghosts is easily Walkers most calm, sparse, and intimate album. After first listen it is immediately apparent it is an album he had to make. Album climax "Father's Day" tells you all you need to know about the albums content: "Sunday Morning, Father's Day / The first without my dad / As I look into my little boy's eyes / It takes all I have / Not to break down right in front of him when he smiles at me / You don't become a man / Until you lose your dad, you see."

Musically, Walker and Adams work together and bring out what each of them does best. The piano-tinged folk of the title track and the steel-pedal quietness of "How are things, Love?" give way to the mellow side of classic rock found in "I love you" or the cathartic guitar solo closing out "Father's Day." Walker is a man who dresses up in many musical costumes, but Afraid of Ghosts is a welcome change into his everyday threads as he reflects on the important aspects of life.

Run Away With Me

Cary Rae Jepsen: Run Away With Me

Carly Rae Jepsen: EMOTION

"We had the biggest single in the world last time and didn't have the biggest album. This time we wanted to stop worrying about singles and focus on having a critically acclaimed album." Think about that statement as you hear the synthesized battlecry that kicks off opening track "Run Away With Me" and launches you into the pop perfection that is Jepsen's EMOTION. Wait, why are you laughing? I am completely serious. I freaking love this album.

The above quote is from Jepsen's manager Scooter Braun and it very clearly explains Jepsen's intention of breaking from the viscous one-hit-wonder cycle that has threatened her after "Call Me Maybe" sprung out of Canada and took my heart by storm (I kid, but that song was great). The Jepsen team challenged the world's best pop minds to show them what they got and the result is a solid and spotless album. More than 200 tracks were workshopped and only 12 ended up making the cut. When you are turning down tracks from Max Martin and Jack Antonoff you know the competition is fierce

While the lyrical content may veer in various different directions and ultimately be about nothing important at all, each songs has a mood of triumph as we see the best of the winning writers and producers. The hit-list includes: Shellback (go look at his song credits, it's absurd), Rostam Batmanlij (Vampire Weekend), Dev Hynes (Blood Orange and everything else that is awesome), Sia (from the band Sia), and Daniel Nigro (please come back As Tall As Lions).

The production continuously hits hard, but with so many different voices it maintains a volume of spontaneity that adds to the perfectly structured pop. It never gets stuck in one spot as it whirls through decades of pop music. What I am trying to say is take a listen and have some fun. I have a feeling you will really really really really really really like it.

Sunday Candy

Donnie Trumpet & The Social Experiment: Sunday Candy

Donnie Trumpet & The Social Experiment: Surf

When Chance the Rapper broke out in 2013 behind the critically praised Acid Rap he earned himself a major platform to do exactly what it is he wanted to do. Instead of labeling up and taking the big time advance to release mixtape after mixtape Chicago's favorite son decided to use this outlet on projects that support his ambitious and creative process. One of these outlets is the fantastic Surf.

Donnie Trumpet & The Social Experiment are a self-proclaimed collective of bohemian musicians and consists of Donnie himself, Chance and numerous other Chicago musicians. Hearing a group with the name experiment talking about being a collective means you are going to get a ton of ideas and that is what we get in Surf. Trying to map out the featured vocalists and musicians on this album is a fairly monumental task. Alongside hip-hop stalwarts J Cole, Busta Rhymes, and Erykah Badu you have artists like Big Sean and names hardly known outside of Chicago.

None of this comes off as superficially or muddy though. Anytime you hear a member discuss this album they mention family and how fun and creative the album was. Chance has been running through a "Family Matters" tour where the Social Experiment is his backing band and this structure of family was mentioned numerous times. The songs dance around throughout the album, but that is what makes it fun and a true experiment. It doesn't take itself too seriously.

The glue that keeps all these artists together is the musicianship and production. Overall the album is grounded in soul ranging from 60's to the early naughts, but also touches on disco, 80's funk, and 70's easy listening. Despite the long list of vocalists it isn't really a rap album at all. The unique instrumentation is center stage while the MCs verses add in on the fun.

I would be remiss not to mention lead single "Sunday Candy." The track features a giddy Chance rapping over the Social Experiment at their best. The video consists of a full-fledged stage production that unfolds in front of you in a single take. Colorful sets, backup dancers, the whole deal. It is also visual proof that the family works hard and has fun doing it.

Muse: Drones

Reapers

Muse: Reapers

Muse: Drones

It speaks to the pretentiousness and grandiosity of Matt Bellamy when he mentions that the bands 7th studio album Drones would be their chance to "reconnect and remind ourselves of just the basics of who we are," only to drop a gargantuan concept album about human drones in the literal sense as well as a metaphorical representation of the lack of empathy in the modern world. Yeah, exactly, it's a mouthful, but what aspect of Muse hasn't been?

In a time where there are very few true, full-fledged rockstars left in music it is encouraging to continue to see Bellamy and Co. crank up the volume to 11, ramp up the production, and craft songs for the soul purpose of playing them in arenas around the world. I don't listen to Muse the way I listen to a lot of other music, you don't listen to Muse for lyrical deepness, we all listen to Muse to rock the eff out and air guitar to Bellamy's brilliance.

With The Resistance and The 2nd Law Muse got drowned in their own ambition and released two lackluster albums that drudged in their bold attempt at electro-symphonic bliss. Let's not speak too poorly on The 2nd Law though, because "Madness" might be the best song that has ever been written in the history of all time.

What Muse meant when they mused on (Sorry: had to do it) about returning to their roots is dropping the dub, the orchestra, the nonsense and picking back up the guitar, bass, and drum kit. That decision is what makes this album so fun and so full of rock you will think nothing of the awful lyrics and political conquest that lies within. Lead single "Psyhco" packs a Zeppelin-esque riff that the crew has been playing during interludes live for years. It marches on with a purpose and never lets go. "Reapers" continues the bluesy heavy metal with a thesis on guitar rock put on from Bellamy starting at the 2:40 mark.

The overblown theatrics of Queen are not entirely forgotten on "Revolt" and "The Globalist." One of the reasons I love "Madness" so damn much is that after the quick and powerful guitar solo the song morphs into an all-time bridge that Muse does better than anyone else. Opener "Dead Inside" features on of those bridges that harkens back to Absolution and adds in the synth-pop of Depeche Mode. "The Handler" is absolute classic Muse and what I have wanted to hear from them for years: dirty, Black Sabbath riffs, with Bellamy hitting the falsetto.

Bottom line is everyone should turn it to 11, grab that plug in baby and crucify your enemies.

King Kunta

Kendrick Lamar: King Kunta

Kendrick Lamar: To Pimp a Butterfly

I was almost tempted to keep this album off of my list because I simply could not wrap my head around trying to consume all of it and understand how it can be boiled down into a few paragraphs. An album as critically acclaimed as this has been dissected through all corners of the media and the internet and browsing through them made me realize other people were as lost as I am.

Kendrick was at an all-time high after the release of good kid, m.A.A.d city and instead of capitalizing on that and releasing an album full of hooks, guest spots, and viral videos he chose to have us listen in on a 79 minute therapy session. Gone are the easy hooks. Songs get interrupted with arguments and careen out of control. Hip-hop structures have been replaced with a psychedelic onslaught of jazz, soul, and funk. The comfortable feeling of a capable single has to be precisely extracted from the sprawling story. How does today's single-hungry listener experience a 79-minute album that makes you feel uncomfortable at every turn?

To Pimp a Butterfly is a dense and important album and one that touches on so many of the subjects that have made 2015 an uncomfortable year. Kendrick is both petrified by his isolation at the top and inspired by the freedom that it gives him. While the obvious theme of the record is to urge black Americans to rise above racism there is a lot more to it. Faith in god, depression battles, adoration for his country, and self-loathing spiral around stunning musical experiments that seem to have no boundary.

I struggled to understand the renowned adoration for something this unstructured and difficult to consume. Was every media outlet praising this album because it came from a prominent black voice in a crucial time in America? Has anyone truly listened to it all the way through? Not because it isn't good, but because it simply is difficult to do.

The reason it lands on this list is because it displays to me the ability of a major superstar to completely understand his platform, but not cater to a single expectation of what it means to have the title. It is an artist experimenting with his medium and seeing what the consumer can handle.

What it does the most for me is it highlights a possible path for which albums can be consumed in today's landscape. Seeing this album top many year lists and earning an album of the year Grammy nomination makes me want to poll people and ask them how many times they have listened to it full through. 1-5? 10-20? I certainly haven't been able to take it in 20 times. Maybe albums with these types of messages and these ambitions are maybe meant to be consumed like a movie. A handful of times a year you sit down and give your full attention for an hour. This album goes through acts, forces you to think, takes ambitious turns, like any good movie will do. It doesn't quite make sense to break the movie into 16 segments, but like any movie when you come across an incredible scene you take notice and carry that with you. All those experiences are present in this album. That's how I view this album and it makes me more excited to see what else can happen when artists like Kendrick choose to speak and share with us our creativity.

I do hope some part of that made sense.

Used To

MUTEMATH: Used To

MUTEMATH: Vitals

MUTEMATH has never been a band to settle for too long. They lie among so many various musical fault lines that they never quite coalesce into a particular style or scene long enough to get them too much attention. Grammy nominations or not, they are somewhat of an outsider to their alt-rock contemporaries and Vitals sees the New Orleans Quartet shapeshifting once again.

MUTHEMATHs trajectory began with math-rock takes on radio-ready anthems and saw it morph into the bluesy, garage rock of 2010's Odd Soul On Vitals MUTEMATH have opted for a more synth driven, electro-pop centered around their trademarked experimentation and the result is their most infectious album to date.

The common thread that has allowed the band to stay on track during their numerous wanderings is that the songs are tactfully constructed and always have an ear towards pop. Everything is excellently poised, controlled, and never overdone. The opening trio of songs displays a tour-de-force of MUTEMATH's fresh take on synth pop. "Light Up" delivers a chorus with a wobbly bass and synthetic snare that effortlessly transitions into shimmering synths and handclaps. Lead single "Monument" has upbeat drums contrasted against a diving guitar riff.

From here the band mixes up the pace of the album and dips into the experimentation and rhythms that longtime fans have grown to love. This unpredictability is what makes the songs adventurous, weird, fun, and bottom line a joy to listen to. It is nice to hear Josh having some fun again.

Let It Happen

Tame Impala: Let It Happen

Tame Impala: Currents

Tame Impala exists purely in the mind of Kevin Parker down to every note in each song and every light in each live performance. After two critically acclaimed albums and rise to festival stardom Tame Impala could have easily rested on their psych-rock, guitar-driven talent, but that was simply not going to be the case. As the opening notes of album opener "Let it Happen" envelope you, Parker unveils the future and sweeps you up into a world of change. At about the halfway mark the song seems to kick into gear and catch you off guard. The track clicks and repeats itself and before you can question it you quickly realize that it's a brilliantly crafted segue into the pulsating and propulsive second act. The song sprawls over a psych-rock disco and is easily Parker's most production-heavy piece. As the track alternates between styles, volumes, and focus points it features a meticulous obsession that has become trademark Parker. "Let it happen" is about "Finding yourself always in this world of chaos...always shutting it out because you don't want to be part of it. But at some point, you realize it takes more energy to shut it out than it does to let it happen and be a part of 'it'."

The song also serves as words of comfort to just trust Parker and accept this change. Currents finds Parker ushering us into a world of change washed over with synths and disco beats. While less guitar driven, the trademark phasing effects, echoing vocals, and other prog touches make for an album that remains psychedelic as ever. It is dance music for closed eyes and slow grooves.

One of the most interesting elements of the album is its pop focus. Parker worked closely with Mark Ronson on Uptown Special and maybe time spent there has seeped into the consciousness of Tame Impala. The opening of "The Moment" recalls 80's Michael Jackson. Where we might have previously witnessed a guitar wig-out or psychedelic drop, Parker infuses in slick melodies and finger clicks over traditional pop structures that we rarely see from the band.

The central focus of change is perfectly encapsulated in "Yes, I'm Changing." The swirling electro ballad is the closest the band will ever get to a diva-style power track. What makes Parkers take on the ballad so interesting is that he shrouds if over a sonic cloud as he lazily delivers the heartbreaking realization that "they say people never change but that's bullshit / They do."

Currents is an album that finds itself easy to dance to, but at the same time it is a deeply melancholic albums as well. "Eventually" approaches the breakup song from the rare perspective of the person doing the breakup. "And I know just what I've got to do / And it's got to be soon / Cause I know that I'll be happier / And I know you will too." It is a powerful song that features a rare barrage of fuzz before a single finger snap brings you back to the disco. Relationships are just another facet of change that Parker touches on. The difference here is Parker's approach. 2012's Lonerism featured schizophrenic introspection that constantly drew in on itself. Currents reverses that course and approaches many of the same issues with hopeful optimism.

Tame Impala have provided us with a masterpiece of an album that serves best as a sonic tour. There is no apparent beginning and end, just a world you weren't aware you could be carried off into. Just let it happen and you will quickly realize you are all the better for it.

Jackrabbit

San Fermin: Jackrabbit

San Fermin: Jackrabbit

San Fermin is a Brooklyn collective born out of the mind of classically trained composer Ellis Ludwig-Leone. As a live outfit they have settled in as an octet that features Ludwig-Leone standing way to the side of the stage on his keyboards as he lets the rest of the crew jump around vocal duties and instruments that include saxophone, trumpet, guitar, drums, violins, and whatever else they can bang on. Seeing Ludwig-Leone on the side of the stage like a director and knowing he is responsible for each and every note is really interesting to watch. He is extremely humble, when the band members mention he writes the music he always has a visual cringe. There is a clear sense of joy in his body language as he gets to hang out with his best friends and watch them enjoy themselves over the music that means so much to him. This kind of composer-performer relationship is nothing new in music, but it is very rare in this branch of the industry and because of that it is extremely refreshing.

The driving force of the band is the juxtaposed male and female voices of Allen Tate and Charlene Kaye. It is impossible not to notice Tate is a carbon copy of Matt Berninger of The National, but this does nothing to distract from his performance contrary to what other people might have you believe. His deep, smooth, and rumbling baritone drives the music forward with force, but Kaye offers an angelic and rapturously operatic foil to Tate and makes for many special moments throughout the album.

The vocal dynamics on top of highly orchestral music carry you to another dimension. Listening to Ludwig-Leones restless song structuring is indicative of an individual that is simply on a completely different level than the rest of us. Many of the songs start off very classical and traditional but break off into all different directions. There are surreal twilight endings, cacophonous explosions reminiscent of a grand finale, and constantly piercing horns and violin.

The back to back of "Emily" and "Jackrabbit" are what makes the DNA of San Fermin so damn good. "Emily" is driven by Tate where his lyrics and the music express his loneliness, despite being among people, and his pursuit of emotional stability. Immediately after, "Jackrabbit" is fronted by Kaye and acts as a kick in the face and direct response to Tate's sentiment. Ludwig-Leone states: "'Jackrabbit' is the last song I wrote on this record, and it definitely sums up some themes I've been circling for a while now. It's fight-or-flight plus a marching band, which is basically my stance on everything." Such a perfect line to encapsulate what San Fermin is all about.

Should Have Known Better

Sufjan Stevens: Should Have Known Better

Sufjan Stevens: Carrie and Lowell

For as long as Sufjan Stevens has been making music he has consistently conveyed great emotion. There is a consistent mark of melancholy that is masked in an outsized sense of ambition. For every musing on death or grief we find strings, horns, electronic madness, or a choir to help distract ourselves. This was none more apparent than on 2010's Age of Adz when Stevens masks the pains of "articles of imminent death" with saturated trans-genre maximalism. On Carrie and Lowell all the theatrics are stripped and completely hollowed out by grief. What is left behind is an album as expansive as any other Sufjan effort, but achieved without the ambition and vigor of times past.

The album title refers to Stevens' Mother and Stepfather. Carrie left Sufjan and the rest of the family when he was very young. Over the years he got to know his mother over visits to Oregon. She constantly struggled with depression, substance abuse, and schizophrenia. In 2012 she died of cancer and Stevens was around in the months leading up to her death. Carrie and Lowell is the most powerful kind of storytelling. Steven's pulls you into his own sense emotions to create the link between listener and artist. In the process of detailing the mother-son connection that never was, Stevens faces up to the universals of grief, guilt, anger, questions of faith, and the sense of finality and absence. The result is heartbreaking, powerful, moving, and above all hopeful.

Every line in Carrie and Lowell has meaning and is meant to be heard. Instrumentation has been reduced to beautiful melodies of acoustic guitar and piano. The production is so bare you can hear air conditioners in the background and Sufjan exhaling from the weight of emotion. It is a delicacy that completely engrosses the listener as Stevens expresses the words he never had the chance to say. "I forgive you mother, I can hear you and I long to be near you, but every road leads to an end" follows a beautiful piano melody on opener "Death with Dignity." It sounds as if Sufjan is sitting in the corner of the room singing only to himself.

Album highlight "Should Have Known Better" might just be my favorite musical moment of 2015. It is a stunning story told in two parts. The first half takes us back to the exact moments Carrie left the kids at a video store. It is sung in a hushed voice that finds Steven's grasping for warmth in the wake of her absence. At the halfway mark an uplifting electric keyboard kicks in and subtle notes of percussion tap into view to remind us to keep going. The vocals form a chorus with themselves to shake off the shadow and bloom into something hopeful. The song discovers a positive perspective. Fear is shoved aside and Sufjan begins to see the beauty of his mother in his daughter-in-law's face. I should have known better / Nothing can be changed / The past is still the past / The bridge to nowhere is sung not with resigned pessimism, but with the easiness that comes from letting go of the past and embracing the present. It is one of those incredible moments that makes listening to music so enjoyable.

The album is riddled with honesty and a descriptive wrestling of emotion. In the tender and visceral "Fourth of July" Sufjan imagines a conversation with his mother from the grave that is mostly spoken from her perspective. She apologized for leaving the family, asks him not to cry, and questions if he felt enough love. "Did you get enough love, my little dove / Why do you cry? / And I'm sorry I left, but it was for the best / Though it never felt right." The song ends with the eerie repetition of "We're all gonna die." On album closer "Blue Bucket of Gold" Sufjan takes us back to the most basic themes of human life: the desire to be loved and our own mortality. "Tell me you want me in your life, or raise your red flag, just as I want you in my life."

What really set this album apart from any others this year was witnessing it in a live setting. At the Copley Symphony Hall in San Diego I had the chance to see Sufjan for the first time. What followed was two hours of one of the most cathartic and moving experiences in my entire life. Joined by a full band Stevens went through the entirety of the album without saying a word and letting the music speak for itself. Every song was reworked to reach new heights with the band backing and added in features of past Sufjan music to make it an entirely new experience. At the halfway mark, before launching into some fan favorites, Sufjan spoke to the crowd with an honesty of two friends talking to each other. Interlaced with stories about his mother he thanked us for letting him be there and letting him express this period of his life through the music. The final song of the night featured a ten minute version of "Blue Bucket of Gold" that was utterly stunning and beautiful. At the end Sufjan simply said "I love you" to the crowd and never before have I heard those words delivered with such honesty and meaning.

Listening to an album with such a backstory, so much emotion, and so much room to explore is what makes diving into good music so rewarding. Couple that with a tremendous live performance that presents the music in an entirely different light and you have just a small glimpse into what makes music so special to me and such a joy to spend time with and consume.
